## 01 — Brand Voice Guide

fitted. speaks like a senior mentor who's seen enough hiring cycles to know what actually matters — and respects you enough to say it plainly. The voice is calm, specific, and slightly contrarian. It never cheerleads. It never sells urgency. It doesn't need to.

On social, that means leading with an observation or tension that's true and a little uncomfortable — something a thoughtful person in a job search would recognize immediately but rarely hears said out loud. The copy is spare. The headline does the work. Body text deepens the idea; it doesn't repeat it. We write for the person who is scrolling slowly and carefully, not for the one who needs to be grabbed.

**What we never sound like:** A LinkedIn influencer celebrating their "wins." A career coach with a five-step framework. A SaaS product promising transformation. We don't use exclamation points. We don't create FOMO. We don't say "you've got this," "dream job," "crush it," or any variation of urgency theater. If a line could appear on a motivational poster, it doesn't belong to fitted.

## 02 — Reusable Content Templates

---

### Template 1 — The Uncomfortable Truth

**When to use:** When a widely held belief about job searching is wrong, incomplete, or being politely avoided. Best for Instagram feed carousels and standalone reels. High save rate.

**Structure (Carousel — 5 slides):**
- **Slide 1 (Hook):** A single, declarative statement that names the tension. No qualifier. No softening. Garamond serif, large, navy on off-white or white on navy.
- **Slide 2 (The Diagnosis):** Two or three sentences that name what's actually happening and why. DM Sans body, off-white background.
- **Slide 3 (The Shift):** The reframe. What changes when you see it differently. One insight, not a list.
- **Slide 4 (The Specifics):** What this looks like in practice. Concrete, not abstract. One or two examples.
- **Slide 5 (Closing + CTA):** Approved CTA line. "fitted." wordmark bottom-right. Taupe-pale background.

**Recommended typography:**
- Slide 1: EB Garamond / display size (48–56px equivalent) / navy or off-white
- Slides 2–4: DM Sans body / 15–17px / muted `#6b7280` body, navy `#2f3e5c` for emphasis
- Slide 5: EB Garamond italic for CTA / DM Sans for "fitted." label

**Example headline:** *"Your resume is describing your job. It should be describing your judgment."*

**First line of body (slide 2):** "Most resumes are a list of responsibilities. Hiring managers already know what a product manager does — they want to see what you decided, what you changed, and what you owned when things went sideways."

---

### Template 2 — The Quiet Data Point

**When to use:** When a statistic or behavioral pattern reveals something surprising about how hiring actually works. Excellent for short reels (15–30 seconds) and square feed posts. Designed to stop a scroll cold without shouting.

**Structure (Reel / Single Frame):**
- **Opening frame:** Large Garamond number or percentage, centered. Navy on off-white. No context yet.
- **Frame 2:** The stat, contextualized in one sentence. What it means for the viewer.
- **Frame 3 (or caption):** The implication — what you'd do differently if you took this seriously.
- **Close:** "fitted." wordmark, taupe label, approved CTA line.

**Recommended typography:**
- Stat: EB Garamond / display-xl (72px+) / navy
- Context: DM Sans / 13–15px / muted
- Implication: EB Garamond italic / 20–24px / navy
- Label: DM Sans / uppercase / taupe / 0.12em tracking

**Example headline (the stat):** *"6 seconds."*

**First line of body:** "The average recruiter's first pass on a resume. Not because they're careless — because most resumes give them nothing to slow down for."

---

### Template 3 — The Reframe

**When to use:** When you want to take a common career anxiety and replace it with a more useful way of seeing. Pairs well with the brand's role as a quiet mentor. Best for carousels and saved-for-later posts.

**Structure (Carousel — 4 slides):**
- **Slide 1 (Old frame):** State the conventional wisdom. No comment yet. Light, almost neutral. Off-white background, muted DM Sans.
- **Slide 2 (The crack):** Where that framing breaks down. One specific, honest observation.
- **Slide 3 (New frame):** The alternative perspective, stated simply. Garamond serif, slightly larger, more confident.
- **Slide 4 (Implication + CTA):** What changes practically. Approved CTA. fitted. wordmark.

**Recommended typography:**
- Slide 1: DM Sans / light weight 300 / muted tone — the conventional wisdom shouldn't feel authoritative
- Slide 2: DM Sans / 400 / navy — the crack is serious
- Slide 3: EB Garamond / italic / navy — the reframe earns the serif
- Slide 4: DM Sans / 400 / muted body + EB Garamond CTA line

**Example headline (slide 3):** *"The question isn't whether you're qualified. It's whether the role fits where you're actually going."*

**First line of body (slide 2):** "Chasing the highest match score or the most prestigious company name is a way of outsourcing the decision. The candidates who move fastest usually know exactly what kind of work they want to do next — and filter for that first."

---

### Template 4 — The fitted. Thinks

**When to use:** When you want to demonstrate the product's voice and point of view — candid, specific, a little uncomfortable in the best way. This template directly previews what the in-app "fitted. thinks" insight experience feels like. Works for carousels, quote-style frames, and short reels.

**Structure (Single frame or 2-slide carousel):**
- **Frame / Slide 1:** The "fitted. thinks" label in DM Sans uppercase taupe. Below it, an insight in EB Garamond italic — one to three sentences. Taupe-pale or navy background.
- **Slide 2 (optional):** What this means for the viewer's search. One practical implication. DM Sans body.

**Recommended typography:**
- Label: DM Sans / 11px / uppercase / taupe `#b8a99a` / 0.12em tracking
- Insight: EB Garamond / italic / 20–28px / navy (on light) or off-white (on navy)
- Body: DM Sans / 14–15px / muted

**Example headline:** fitted. thinks

**First line of body:** *"You've applied to 14 roles in the last 60 days. Eleven of them were at the same two companies. That's not a job search — it's a preference disguised as a strategy."*

---

### Template 5 — The Before / After

**When to use:** To demonstrate the product's core value proposition — resume tailoring and AI guidance — without being demo-y or salesy. The contrast should be specific enough that someone in a job search feels seen. Best as a carousel or reel.

**Structure (Carousel — 3–4 slides):**
- **Slide 1 (Before):** A real-sounding resume bullet or application behavior. Honest, slightly painful. Navy or off-white background, DM Sans.
- **Slide 2 (What's missing):** The specific gap — not "this is bad," but "here's what a hiring manager actually needed to see." Candid, not harsh.
- **Slide 3 (After):** The improved version. Specific. Noticeably better. EB Garamond for the improved line, signaling craft.
- **Slide 4 (CTA):** Approved CTA + fitted. wordmark.

**Recommended typography:**
- Before bullet: DM Sans / 14px / muted — deliberately understated
- Gap explanation: DM Sans / 400 / navy — clear and direct
- After bullet: EB Garamond / italic / navy — the serif signals the upgrade
- CTA slide: EB Garamond closing line + DM Sans "fitted." label

**Example headline (slide 2):** *"The scope is there. The decision isn't."*

**First line of body:** "Managed cross-functional team to launch product" tells a recruiter nothing they couldn't assume. What they actually want to know: what did you decide when the product team and engineering disagreed? What changed because you were in the room?"

## 03 — Content Pillar Strategy

---

### Pillar 1 — The Honest Search
*Realism about how hiring actually works, without cynicism.*

The job search is full of myths that cost people time and confidence. This pillar names them plainly, from the perspective of someone who's watched the process carefully. It's not pessimistic — it's clarifying. The audience feels seen, not talked down to.

**Post ideas:**
- "Why your application disappeared (it has nothing to do with your qualifications)" — the reality of ATS filtering and recruiter volume
- "The companies that ghost you are telling you something" — what responsiveness signals about culture before you join
- "There's a difference between being underqualified and being misaligned" — reframing the rejection that stings most

---

### Pillar 2 — Resume as Argument
*The resume is a persuasive document, not a record of employment.*

Most people treat their resume as a transcript. The highest-performing resumes are arguments — they make a specific case for a specific kind of role. This pillar teaches that reframe in pieces, one insight at a time, and previews what fitted.'s resume optimization actually does.

**Post ideas:**
- "Your resume is describing your job. It should be describing your judgment." — the decisions vs. responsibilities distinction
- "Three sentences that will earn a callback, and the three that won't" — specific before/after examples using real resume language
- "The resume that got ignored vs. the one that got the interview" — same experience, different framing

---

### Pillar 3 — The Intentional Career
*For people who are moving toward something, not just away from something.*

This pillar is for the career changers, the returners, the people mid-search who've lost clarity on what they actually want. It's quieter and more personal than the other pillars. Less tactical, more philosophical — but always grounded in something specific. This is where fitted.'s inclusive spirit lives.

**Post ideas:**
- "Intentional next step > optimal next step" — the difference between optimizing for prestige and searching for fit
- "What career changers know that career climbers don't" — the advantages of having a wider frame of reference
- "The job search question nobody asks" — not "am I qualified?" but "is this where I want to spend the next three years of my attention?"

---

### Pillar 4 — What Recruiters Actually See
*Behind-the-scenes intelligence from the other side of the table.*

This pillar is for the mildly contrarian, data-curious audience. It humanizes the hiring process without excusing its flaws, and gives viewers genuine intelligence they can act on. High save and share potential because it feels like privileged information even when it isn't.

**Post ideas:**
- "A recruiter's first 6 seconds on your resume — what they're looking for, in order" — specific hierarchy of attention
- "The cover letter question: does anyone actually read them?" — honest answer with nuance
- "Why the candidate with 80% of the qualifications sometimes gets the role" — the logic of "culture add" and initiative signals

---

### Pillar 5 — The fitted. POV
*Where the brand speaks with a direct editorial voice.*

Occasional, high-quality takes on the broader career landscape — not trend-chasing, but thoughtful observation about how work is changing. This pillar establishes fitted. as a voice worth following independent of the product. Sparingly used. Each post earns its place.

**Post ideas:**
- "The résumé will probably survive AI — but the one-size-fits-all approach won't" — on personalization as the durable skill
- "Quiet quitting was never about effort. It was about alignment." — a retroactive read on what that cultural moment actually revealed
- "The six-month job search is a data point, not a character flaw" — on timing, volume, and the randomness of hiring pipelines
